What is Natural Language Processing?

Natural Language Processing (NLP) refers to the branch of AI that gives machines the ability to read, understand, and derive meaning from human languages. It combines linguistics and CS to decipher language structures, guidelines, and to make models that can comprehend, break down, and separate significant details from text and speech. NLP combines computational linguistics—rule-based modeling of human language—with statistical, machine learning, and deep learning models. Together, these technologies enable computers to process human language in the form of text or voice data and to ‘understand’ its full meaning, complete with the speaker or writer’s intent and sentiment. Some quick examples of NLP include autocorrect, plagiarism checkers searching through the web, and robots answering the phone.

The skillful use of language, like slurring speech, abbreviating, and leaving out words, makes us human. Computers, on the other hand, only understands language that adheres to strict syntax rules (like when we code). NLP emerged as a result of human’s desire for computers to speak and understand the complexity of our language, and it strives to build machines that understand and respond to text or voice data—and respond with text or speech of their own—in much the same way humans do.

How Does NLP Work?

Most techniques used in NLP are simple grammar techniques we’ve been taught in school.

The process of NPL can be broken down into three main parts:

1. Speech-to-Text: The computer performs a speech recognition routine that converts the natural language to a programming language.

2. Part-of-Speech: This process elementarily identifies words in their grammatical forms using a set of lexicon rules coded into the computer and tags the each word with its part-of-speech.

(After these two processes, the computer probably now understands the meaning of the speech that was made.)

3. Text-to-Speech conversion: The computer programming language is converted into an audible or textual format for the user.

More Specific Processes:

1. Segmentation: the input is broken down into its constituent sentences using punctuation

2. Tokenizing: sentences are broken into its constituent words (tokens)

3. Third: Removing stop words: non-essential works like are, in, and, the are removed

4. Stemming: computer recognizes that words like skipping, skips, and skip are the same word

5. Lemmatization: base words for word tenses like mood and gender are identified

6. Tagging: NLP tags each word with its part-of-speech

7. Named entity tagging: the computer is introduced to pop culture references and everyday names
